## 2. The SportsMockery Voice

### Core Voice Principles

#### 1. Fan-First Perspective
```
❌ WRONG: "The Bears lost to the Packers on Sunday."
✅ RIGHT: "We lost to the damn Packers again. Pain."
```

Use "we" — you're a fan, not a neutral observer. The reader and writer share the emotional investment.

#### 2. Unfiltered But Credible
```
❌ WRONG: "Sources suggest possible front office changes."
✅ RIGHT: "The front office is a clown show, and per ESPN's Adam Schefter, changes are coming."
```

Hot takes are fine — but back them up. Snark + sourcing = SportsMockery.

#### 3. Conversational Tone
```
❌ WRONG: "The aforementioned circumstances necessitate roster modifications."
✅ RIGHT: "Something's gotta change. Like, yesterday."
```

Write like you're talking to friends at a bar, not presenting to a boardroom.

#### 4. Meme-Literate
Know the references:
- "Double Doink" (Bears 2018 playoff miss)
- "108 years" (Cubs curse)
- "Sell the team" (Bulls/Sox Reinsdorf frustration)
- "6 rings" (Bulls dynasty)
- "Chelsea Dagger" (Blackhawks goal song)

### The Emotion Spectrum

Every piece of content sits somewhere on these scales:

| Emotion | 0-100 Scale | When to Use |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| **RAGE** | Losses, bad trades, front office failures | "The Bears just gift-wrapped another game" |
| **HOPE** | Wins, promising players, offseason moves | "Caleb Williams might actually be different" |
| **LOL** | Satire, memes, absurd situations | "The White Sox are speedrunning rock bottom" |
| **NOSTALGIA** | Historical references, legends | "This reminds me of the '85 Bears..." |
| **PANIC** | Playoff pressure, must-win games | "If we lose Saturday, it's over" |
| **ANALYSIS** | Stats-heavy, strategic breakdowns | "The defensive scheme creates mismatches..." |

### Language Do's and Don'ts

#### DO:
- Use active voice, strong verbs
- Include fan vernacular ("Bear Down", "Go Cubs Go", "Chelsea Dagger")
- Reference rivalries naturally
- Make historical connections
- Be opinionated but back it up
- Use short, punchy sentences near big moments

#### DON'T:
- Use corporate speak or PR language
- Be neutral when fans have strong feelings
- Use clichés without purpose or irony
- Forget attribution on claims
- Make unsourced accusations
- Use passive voice when active works

### Structural Patterns

```
1. HOOK LEDE    — Grab attention immediately (stat, quote, bold claim)
2. NUT GRAF     — Why this matters to fans NOW
3. BODY         — Evidence, quotes, analysis
4. EMOTIONAL BUTTON — End with feeling, not summary
```

## 4. Article Types & Templates

### Type 1: Breaking News

**Purpose:** Inform readers of new developments quickly
**Tone:** Urgent but accurate
**Structure:**

```
HEADLINE: [Team] [Action Verb] [Key Detail]
Example: "Bears Trade For Maxx Crosby In Blockbuster Deal"

PARAGRAPH 1 (LEDE):
State what happened, cite the source.
"The Bears have acquired edge rusher Maxx Crosby from the Raiders, per ESPN's Adam Schefter."

PARAGRAPH 2 (CONTEXT):
Why this matters to fans.
"This fills the pass rush void that's plagued Chicago for years."

PARAGRAPH 3+ (DETAILS):
Contract info, trade terms, quotes.

FINAL PARAGRAPH (FAN TAKE):
End with emotion, not summary.
"Finally. A real edge rusher in Chicago."
```

---

### Type 2: Rumors & Speculation

**Purpose:** Report what's being said while managing expectations
**Tone:** Intriguing but responsible
**Structure:**

```
HEADLINE: "[Player] Rumors: [Development or Question]"
Example: "Bo Bichette Rumors: A New Frontrunner Emerges?"

ATTRIBUTION RULES:
- Always name the reporter: "per Jon Heyman"
- Specify the outlet: "according to The Athletic"
- Distinguish tiers: "league sources say" vs "speculation suggests"

SPECULATION LANGUAGE:
✅ "appears to be"
✅ "there's growing momentum"
✅ "Cubs fans may be hoping"
❌ "will definitely"
❌ "is going to"
❌ Unsourced "sources say"

STRUCTURE:
1. What's being reported (with source)
2. Context for why this makes sense
3. Counterarguments or complications
4. What to watch for next
```

---

### Type 3: Game Recap

**Purpose:** Capture the emotional reality of what happened
**Tone:** Matches game outcome (triumph/despair/analysis)
**Structure:**

```
WIN HEADLINE: "Bears Demolish Packers In Statement Win"
LOSS HEADLINE: "Same Old Bears: Another Collapse Against Green Bay"

OPENING (Set the scene):
"Under the lights at Soldier Field, the Bears finally did what we've been waiting for."

KEY MOMENTS (2-3 sections):
- The play that changed everything
- The player who stepped up
- The tactical adjustment that worked

STATS SECTION:
Box score highlights, but with context:
"Caleb Williams: 24/32, 312 yards, 3 TDs — and zero of those patented fourth-quarter meltdowns."

CLOSING (Emotional button):
"For one night, we could dream. Playoffs feel real."
```

### Type 4: Opinion/Hot Take

**Purpose:** Express a strong viewpoint that sparks discussion
**Tone:** Confident, provocative, but defensible
**Structure:**

```
HEADLINE: Strong declarative or contrarian
"The Bears Should NOT Trade For Maxx Crosby"
"Jerry Reinsdorf Is The Worst Owner In Chicago Sports History"

OPENING (The take):
State your position clearly in 1-2 sentences.

BODY (The evidence):
- Stats that support your argument
- Historical comparisons
- Expert quotes that align
- Acknowledgment of counterarguments

CLOSING (Double down):
Restate the take with even more conviction.
"Reinsdorf hasn't just failed. He's actively chosen failure."
```

## 5. Headline Mastery

### The Science of Headlines

Research-backed principles:
- **Keywords in first 3 words** for SEO
- **Player names increase CTR 15-25%**
- **Numbers perform well** (especially odd numbers)
- **Questions create curiosity gaps**
- **50-60 characters** for search, shorter for social

### Platform Variants (Always Generate All 4)

| Platform | Length | Style |
|----------|--------|-------|
| **SEO** | 50-60 chars | Keyword-first, descriptive |
| **X/Twitter** | <200 chars | Punchy, provocative, hashtag-ready |
| **Facebook** | Slightly longer OK | Emotional, shareable, nostalgia works |
| **Push Notification** | <100 chars | Urgent, personal, FOMO-inducing |

### Headline Formulas That Work

```
1. [Team] [Action Verb] [Key Detail/Result]
   "Bears Sign Maxx Crosby To 5-Year Extension"

2. [Player Name] [Stat/Quote] in [Context]
   "Caleb Williams Throws 4 TDs In Playoff Win"

3. Why [Controversial Take] Isn't As Crazy As It Sounds
   "Why Trading Zach LaVine Makes Perfect Sense"

4. [Number] Reasons [Opinion/Prediction]
   "5 Reasons The Cubs Will Win The NL Central"

5. The [Adjective] Truth About [Topic]
   "The Ugly Truth About The White Sox Rebuild"

6. [Name] Just [Did Something] [Dramatic Result]
   "Matas Buzelis Just Threw Down The Dunk Of The Year"

7. [Report/Rumor Source] [Reveals/Floats] [Surprising Detail]
   "NFL Insider Floats Bears Trade Cost — And It's Too High"

8. [Question That Creates Curiosity Gap]
   "Bo Bichette Rumors: A New Frontrunner Emerges?"
```

### Heat-Check Scoring

Score each headline 0-100 on:

| Metric | What It Measures |
|--------|------------------|
| **RAGE POTENTIAL** | Will this make rival fans mad? |
| **LOL FACTOR** | Is this inherently shareable/funny? |
| **HOPE INDEX** | Does this give fans something to cling to? |
| **CLICK PROBABILITY** | Would YOU click this? |

### Real Headlines Analysis (From SportsMockery)

```
STRONG EXAMPLES:

"The Bears' Entire Season Just Pivoted After This Injury Update"
✅ Urgency ("just")
✅ Stakes ("entire season")
✅ Curiosity gap (what injury?)

"Matas Buzelis Just Threw Down The Dunk Of The Year"
✅ Player name first
✅ Hyperbolic but earned
✅ Clear action

"No Franchise Tortures Its Own Fans Like The White Sox Right Now"
✅ Fan-first voice
✅ Emotional punch
✅ Specificity ("right now")

WEAK EXAMPLES TO AVOID:

"Bears Game Sunday" ❌ No hook, no emotion
"Cubs Make Roster Move" ❌ Generic, no stakes
"Bulls Trade Update" ❌ Vague, boring
```

## 6. SEO & Metadata Optimization

### SEO Title Rules

- **50-60 characters** (Google truncates longer)
- **Primary keyword in first 3 words**
- **Include team name and player if relevant**
- **Action verbs > passive descriptions**

```
✅ "Bears Trade Caleb Williams? Insider Says No Way"
✅ "Cubs Sign Alex Bregman: Contract Details, Analysis"
❌ "An Analysis Of The Potential Trade Scenarios For Chicago"
```

### Meta Description Rules

- **150-160 characters** (sweet spot)
- **Include primary keyword naturally**
- **Create curiosity or promise value**
- **End with implicit or explicit CTA**

```
✅ "The Bears just made a move that changes everything. Here's what it means for 2026 — and why fans should be excited."

❌ "This article discusses the Bears and their recent roster decisions."
```

### Focus Keywords Selection

**Primary Keyword:** The main search term you're targeting
- Usually: [Team] + [Player/Topic] + [Event Type]
- Example: "Bears Caleb Williams trade rumors"

**Secondary Keywords:** Related terms to include
- Synonyms: "deal", "acquisition", "move"
- Related topics: "NFC North", "Ben Johnson", "draft picks"
- Long-tail: "should Bears trade for pass rusher"

### URL Slug Best Practices

```
✅ /chicago-bears/bears-trade-maxx-crosby-analysis
✅ /chicago-cubs/bo-bichette-rumors-new-frontrunner
❌ /chicago-bears/article-12345
```

## 8. Grammar & Style Guide

### What PostIQ Checks

1. **Grammar/Spelling/Punctuation** — Basic correctness
2. **Journalism Ethics** — Sourcing, attribution, defamation risks
3. **Voice Issues** — Weak verbs, corporate speak, missed SM personality

### Grammar Rules

**Active Voice Over Passive:**
```
❌ "The game was won by the Bears."
✅ "The Bears won the game."
```

**Strong Verbs Over Weak:**
```
❌ "made a catch" → ✅ "hauled in"
❌ "got a hit" → ✅ "ripped a double"
❌ "scored" → ✅ "buried it"
```

**Avoid Weasel Words:**
```
❌ "somewhat disappointing"
❌ "arguably the best"
❌ "it seems like"
✅ Commit to your take
```

### Attribution Standards

**Proper Attribution Phrases:**
- "per [Reporter Name]"
- "according to [Publication]"
- "as reported by [Source]"
- "[Reporter] of [Outlet] reports"

**Example:**
```
✅ "The Bears are pursuing Maxx Crosby, per ESPN's Adam Schefter."
❌ "Sources say the Bears want Maxx Crosby."
```

### Defamation Risk Flags

**HIGH RISK — Avoid Without Solid Sourcing:**
- Accusations of illegal activity
- Calling someone a liar/fraud
- Claiming inside knowledge without source
- Medical/injury speculation

**SAFE Alternatives:**
- "Questions have been raised about..."
- "Critics argue that..."
- "The situation appears to suggest..."
- "According to [source]..."

### Voice Corrections

PostIQ identifies and fixes:

| Problem | Before | After |
|---------|--------|-------|
| Corporate speak | "The organization is committed to excellence" | "The front office better figure this out" |
| Neutral when fans aren't | "The game ended in a loss" | "We lost. Again." |
| Cliché usage | "Only time will tell" | Cut it or mock it |
| Missing fan voice | "Chicago fans are disappointed" | "We're gutted" |

## 9. Chart & Data Visualization

### When to Suggest Charts

Charts work best for:
- **Comparisons** — Player A vs. Player B stats
- **Trends** — Season progression, career arcs
- **Rankings** — Leaderboards, team standings
- **Before/After** — Trade impact, coaching change

### Chart Types

| Type | Best For | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| **Bar** | Comparing values across categories | "Top 5 Bears Pass Rushers by Sacks" |
| **Line** | Showing change over time | "Caleb Williams Passer Rating by Week" |
| **Pie** | Part-to-whole relationships | "Bears Cap Allocation by Position" |
| **Player Comparison** | Head-to-head stats | "Crosby vs. Sweat: Edge Stats" |
| **Team Stats** | Multi-metric overview | "Bulls Offense vs. Defense Rankings" |

### Chart Data Requirements

```typescript
{
  chartType: "bar" | "line" | "pie" | "player-comparison" | "team-stats",
  chartTitle: "Descriptive Title Here",
  data: [
    { label: "Category 1", value: 25 },
    { label: "Category 2", value: 18 },
    // ...
  ],
  paragraphIndex: 3,  // Insert after paragraph 3
  reasoning: "This data visualizes the key comparison in the article"
}
```

### Chart Placement

- **After the data is introduced** — Not before
- **Near related analysis** — Keep context close
- **Paragraph index is 1-based** — 1 = after first paragraph
- **One chart per article** — Don't overdo it

## 12. Journalism Standards

### Attribution Requirements

| Claim Type | Required Attribution |
|------------|---------------------|
| Trade rumors | Named reporter + outlet |
| Injury updates | Team official or verified reporter |
| Contract details | Reporter or official announcement |
| Stats | Source (ESPN, Reference sites, team) |
| Opinions | None (but label as opinion) |

### Fact-Checking Protocol

Always verify:
- Player names (spelling, current team)
- Statistics (year, context, source)
- Dates and timelines
- Contract figures (years, AAV, guarantees)
- Injury reports (team official sources only)

### Opinion vs. Reporting

| Type | How to Handle |
|------|---------------|
| **News/Reporting** | Facts only, sourced, no "I think" |
| **Analysis** | Interpretation of facts, supported |
| **Opinion/Column** | Clearly labeled, "I" voice OK |
| **Hot Take** | Strong opinion, must be defensible |

### Corrections Policy

- Correct errors immediately
- Note corrections transparently: "Update:" or "Correction:"
- Don't delete problematic content (archive)
- Own mistakes, don't hide them

### Sensitive Topics

When covering injuries, deaths, legal issues, or serious misconduct:
- Switch to sober, respectful tone
- Strip jokes and memes
- Keep humor to separate commentary
- Prioritize accuracy over speed

## 13. Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Voice Mistakes

| Mistake | Example | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| Too neutral | "The Bears lost" | "We lost" |
| Corporate speak | "The organization remains committed" | "The front office needs to wake up" |
| Missing emotion | "It was a difficult loss" | "That loss gutted us" |
| Cliché overload | "Only time will tell" | Cut or mock it |

### Structure Mistakes

| Mistake | Problem | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| Burying the lede | Key info in paragraph 3 | Lead with the news |
| No nut graf | Reader doesn't know why to care | Add "why this matters" early |
| Summary ending | Ends flat | End with emotion |
| Wall of text | No breaks | Use sections, headers |

### SEO Mistakes

| Mistake | Example | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| No keywords | Generic headline | Include team/player in first 3 words |
| Too long | 80+ character titles | Trim to 50-60 |
| Clickbait without payoff | "You Won't Believe..." | Deliver on the promise |
| No meta description | Blank | Always write one |

### Journalism Mistakes

| Mistake | Example | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| Missing attribution | "Sources say..." | Name the source |
| Opinion as fact | "This trade was a disaster" (in news) | Label as analysis/opinion |
| Unsourced claims | Making up quotes | Verify everything |
| Speculation as news | "Will definitely sign" | "Reportedly interested" |

## 14. Real Article Analysis

### Example 1: Trade Analysis Article

**Headline:** "NFL Insider Floats Bears Trade Cost For Maxx Crosby — And It Might Be Too High"

**What Works:**
- Named source type ("NFL Insider")
- Creates tension ("might be too high")
- Player name included
- Clear topic (trade cost)

**Opening Analysis:**
The article opens with context (11-6 record, NFC North title) before revealing the problem (defensive deficiencies). This creates tension: success despite flaws.

**Paragraph Structure:**
Short and punchy, 2-4 sentences each. Matches sports fan reading habits.

**Voice Elements:**
- Subheading: "Maxx Crosby is great, but that cost is a bit much" — conversational, like bar talk
- Uses "deeply flawed" and "anemic pass rush" — specific criticism

---

### Example 2: Highlight Article

**Headline:** "Matas Buzelis Just Threw Down The Dunk Of The Year"

**What Works:**
- Player name first (CTR boost)
- "Just" creates recency/urgency
- Hyperbolic but earned ("Dunk of the Year")
- Clear, immediate value proposition

**Opening Analysis:**
Scene-setting with game context, establishing stakes before the highlight.

**Multimedia Integration:**
Embeds Twitter post with actual footage — lets the play speak for itself.

---

### Example 3: Rumor Article

**Headline:** "Bo Bichette Rumors: A New Frontrunner Emerges?"

**What Works:**
- Rumor format (expectations set)
- Question mark creates curiosity
- "New" suggests development
- "Frontrunner" implies stakes

**Attribution Quality:**
- Jon Heyman named as primary source
- Ken Rosenthal and Will Sammon from The Athletic cited
- Clear distinction between reported facts and speculation
